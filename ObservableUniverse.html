Observable Universe

The Observable Universe contains:

  • 10 million superclusters
  • 25 billion galaxy groups
  • 350 billion large galaxies
  • 7 trillion dwarf galaxies
  • and 30 billion trillion (3×10²²) stars

But why is it called the “Observable Universe,” and how do we know it’s 92 billion lightyears across? It’s a bit of a long story. Sit tight, astronaut.

First, scientists have good reason to believe that the universe came into existence 13.8 billion years ago during the Big Bang. This means that light has had 13.8 billion years to travel throughout the universe to reach Earth. Therefore, from our vantage point on Earth, we should be able to see light within a radius of 13.8 billion lightyears.

However, that would mean that we’re able to observe light across a diameter of 27.6 billion lightyears, not 92 billion lightyears. So where does the number 92 come from?

Well, we went from 27.6 billion lightyears to 92 billion lightyears because the universe has expanded that distance since the Big Bang. While the farthest light we can see might have originally come from a point 13.8 billion lightyears away, that same point is now actually 46 billion lightyears away from the Earth. Multiply 46 billion lightyears by 2, and you get 92 billion lightyears.

What lies beyond the Virgo Supercluster? More galaxies and galaxy clusters — billions of them, in fact. You are nearing the end of your intergalactic journey, at the edge of the Observable Universe, which is 92 billion lightyears across.
